02

The Line That Explains the World

In 1805, French mathematician Adrien-Marie Legendre published a paper on predicting comet orbits. Buried in the appendix was a method he called moindres carrés — the method of least squares. Given a set of noisy observations, find the line that minimizes the total squared distance from each point to the line. Four years later, Carl Friedrich Gauss claimed he had been using the same method privately since 1795, and derived it from first principles using probability theory. The priority dispute was acrimonious and unresolved. The method itself was unambiguously revolutionary.

The word "regression" came from a strange discovery by Francis Galton in 1885. He was studying the heights of fathers and sons, and noticed something counterintuitive: exceptionally tall fathers tended to have sons who were tall, but not as tall as the father. The population seemed to "regress" toward the mean across generations. The name stuck — even though what we now call linear regression has nothing to do with Galton's original observation about heights. Naming conventions in statistics are rarely logical.

Linear regression is the foundation of supervised machine learning. Every neural network you will build in this course is, in a certain sense, a very complicated nonlinear regression. Understanding why and how a line is fit to noisy data — what "best" means, what can go wrong, and how to detect it — is the first act of becoming a practitioner.

📐 The Mathematics of Least Squares ▼ expand

Given n data points (x₁,y₁)…(xₙ,yₙ), we fit a line ŷ = β₁x + β₀ by minimizing the Sum of Squared Errors (SSE):

SSE = Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² = Σᵢ (yᵢ − β₁xᵢ − β₀)²

Taking partial derivatives with respect to β₀ and β₁ and setting them to zero gives the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) closed-form solution:

β₁ = Σ(xᵢ − x)(yᵢ − y) / Σ(xᵢ − x)² β₀ = y − β₁ · x

Notice β₀ is defined in terms of the means x and y — this means the OLS line always passes through the centroid (x, y) of the dataset. You can verify this in the demo using the crosshair marker.

Model quality is measured by R² (coefficient of determination):

R² = 1 − SSE / SST    where    SST = Σᵢ (yᵢ − y)²

R² = 1 means perfect fit. R² = 0 means your model is no better than predicting y for every input. R² can go negative if your line is actively worse than that flat baseline.

Why squared, not absolute? Three reasons: (1) squaring is differentiable everywhere, making calculus tractable; (2) it penalizes large errors disproportionately; (3) the OLS solution is unique and expressible in closed form. Absolute-error regression (L1) is harder to solve but more robust to outliers — a tradeoff you will revisit in Era 3 (Regularization).

Lab Exercises

EX 01
📐  The Residual Curtain
Use the demo above — toggle Show Residuals, then Show Squares. Move the line around while both are visible.

Each vertical red line is a residual — the gap between the actual y and your prediction ŷ. Each colored box has side length equal to that residual — so its area equals the squared error. The SSE counter in the panel is literally the total area of all those boxes combined.

→ Move the line to pass through the middle of the cloud. Watch how large outlying boxes shrink while nearby boxes barely change. Why does squaring give outliers so much more influence than nearby points?

→ Now click Show OLS Line. Does the OLS arrangement of squares surprise you? Are they all equal, or does the optimal line still leave some large ones?

Key insight: "Least squares" is not "fewest big errors" — it's a global balance. A line that nails 14 points but catastrophically misses one can have a higher SSE than a line that misses every point moderately. This is why outliers disproportionately distort OLS models.
EX 02
🦎  The Outlier Effect
Drag the amber point and watch the regression line chase it in real time.

The blue points are fixed. Only the amber point moves. The green dashed line shows the regression without the outlier; the orange line shows it with the outlier included.

→ Drag the amber point to an extreme corner. How many standard deviations does the slope shift? Can you reverse the slope direction entirely with one point?

→ In a real dataset with 10,000 rows, a single data-entry error can do the same thing. What's the first thing you should do after fitting a regression in production?

Key insight: OLS is not robust. A point 3× further from the line contributes 9× more to SSE — the squaring gives extreme values enormous leverage. This motivates robust regression, data cleaning (Lesson 3), and L1/L2 regularization (Era 3).

05

Discussion Questions

  • Why Squared? We minimize squared errors rather than absolute errors. Name at least two practical consequences of this choice — one that is a feature and one that is a bug.
  • The Gauss-Legendre Dispute. Both men claimed to have invented least squares. Does priority of publication matter in science? What about in software? Consider: who "invented" the Transformer architecture?
  • Regression to the Mean. Galton found that very tall fathers have shorter sons on average. Is this a genetic force, or a statistical inevitability? If you scored 98/100 on a test, what would you expect on a second attempt — and why?
  • Anscombe Forward. In the deep learning era, we fit extraordinarily complex models. Does Anscombe's lesson still apply? Can a neural network "lie" to you the same way a linear regression can?
  • OLS Assumptions. OLS assumes errors are normally distributed, have constant variance (homoscedasticity), and are uncorrelated. For each assumption, think of a real-world scenario where it would be violated. What would that do to the fitted line and to R²?
06

Terminology

Key terms used in this lesson. Underlined links in the text above jump directly here.

Residual also: error, εᵢ
The vertical distance between an actual data point and the line's prediction at that x value: εᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ. Positive residuals sit above the line; negative ones below. A good fit has residuals that are small, symmetric around zero, and show no obvious pattern. → Used in: SSE, R²
SSE Sum of Squared Errors
The total of every residual squared. Squaring serves two purposes: it makes all errors positive (so they can't cancel), and it penalizes large errors far more harshly than small ones — a point 3× further from the line contributes 9× more to SSE.
SSE = Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
→ See also: OLS (minimizes SSE), R² (normalizes SSE)
OLS Ordinary Least Squares
The closed-form solution that finds the slope (β₁) and intercept (β₀) that minimize SSE exactly — no iteration required. Because it's a calculus solution (set derivatives to zero), it always finds the global optimum. The OLS line has two elegant geometric properties: it always passes through the centroid, and its residuals always sum to exactly zero.
β₁ = Σ(xᵢ−x)(yᵢ−y) / Σ(xᵢ−x)²    β₀ = y − β₁·x
→ Contrast with: gradient descent (Era 4), which iterates toward the same answer
R² Coefficient of Determination
A score from −∞ to 1.0 measuring how much of the variance in y your line actually explains. R² = 1.0 is a perfect fit. R² = 0 means your model does no better than always predicting the mean y. R² < 0 means your line is actively worse than that flat baseline — a sign something has gone seriously wrong.
R² = 1 − SSE / SST    where    SST = Σ(yᵢ − y)²
Warning: Anscombe's Quartet (Ex 03) shows that identical R² values can describe completely different data shapes. R² is a necessary check, not a sufficient one.
Centroid (x, y) — the means
The "center of gravity" of the dataset — the point (x, y) formed by the mean of all x values and the mean of all y values. Every OLS regression line passes exactly through the centroid, regardless of slope. This is a mathematical consequence of the OLS derivation, not a coincidence — it's why the crosshair in the demo always sits on the green line. → Used to derive: OLS intercept β₀ = y − β₁·x
SST Sum of Squares Total
The total variance in y before any model is fit — the SSE you would have if your model were just a flat line at y. SST is the denominator in the R² formula: it sets the baseline for what "100% of the variance" means. SST never changes for a given dataset; only SSE changes as you move the line.
SST = Σ(yᵢ − y)²
