The Cost of Error

Precision and recall are always in tension. The right operating point isn't a statistical optimum — it's a human judgment about which kind of mistake is more acceptable.

02

Two Ways to Be Wrong

In Lesson 5 you learned to read the confusion matrix. Now comes the harder question: once you know your model makes both false positives and false negatives, which type of error should you work harder to avoid?

The answer is never purely mathematical. It is always a value judgment about consequences. A fire alarm and a spam filter are both binary classifiers. They might even have identical confusion matrices on the same dataset. But they should be tuned to completely different operating points — because the cost of a missed fire and the cost of a missed spam email are not remotely comparable.

Precision and recall pull in opposite directions. Lower the decision threshold and more examples get flagged as positive — you catch more true positives (recall goes up) but also flag more negatives incorrectly (precision goes down). Raise the threshold and you only flag when very confident — precision rises but you miss more real positives (recall falls). You cannot maximize both simultaneously. Every operating point is a tradeoff.

"The legal principle 'innocent until proven guilty' is not a statement about probability. It is a deliberate policy choice to prefer false negatives (acquitting the guilty) over false positives (convicting the innocent). The entire criminal justice system is a classifier tuned for high precision at the cost of recall."

This lesson puts that tradeoff in your hands across ten real-world domains. The goal is not to find the "right" answer — it is to understand that the choice of operating point is a moral and practical decision, not a technical one, and that deploying a classifier without thinking carefully about this choice is itself a decision, usually a bad one.

🔴 Optimise for Recall (Minimize FN)

Catch everything real, even at the cost of many false alarms. The right choice when missing a true positive is catastrophic and false alarms are merely inconvenient.

Cancer screening · Smoke alarms · TSA security · Bridge cracks · COVID detection

🟢 Optimise for Precision (Minimize FP)

Only flag when very confident, even at the cost of missing some real positives. The right choice when a false accusation is itself harmful and missed detections are survivable.

Spam filters · Criminal facial recognition · Drug approval · Loan denial

Lab Exercises

EX 01
⚖️ The Threshold Decision
Find appropriate operating points for three scenarios with very different cost profiles.

Select Cancer Screening. Drag the threshold until recall ≥ 0.95 (catching 95%+ of cancers). What happens to precision? How many false positives occur for every true positive you catch?

→ Switch to Spam Filter. Drag until precision ≥ 0.98 (almost no legitimate email lost). What happens to recall? How much spam gets through?

→ Switch to Criminal Facial Recognition. Where should the threshold be — and why is this scenario categorically different from the medical ones? Consider: who bears the cost of a false positive? Is it the same person who benefits from a true positive?

Key insight: The same model — same ROC curve, same AUC — can be appropriate or catastrophic depending entirely on where you set the threshold and who pays for each type of error.

EX 02
📈 ROC vs. PR Curve
Compare what the two curves reveal about each scenario.

Select Cancer Screening (2% prevalence) and note the shape of the PR curve. Now select Spam Filter (40% prevalence). The ROC curves may look similar, but the PR curves will look dramatically different. Why?

→ The PR curve plots Precision (y-axis) vs. Recall (x-axis). For a rare disease at 2% prevalence, even a very good model can have terrible precision at high recall — because there are so many more negatives than positives. The PR curve makes this visible in a way the ROC curve does not.

Key question: When your dataset is severely imbalanced (rare disease, rare fraud), which curve is more informative — ROC or PR? Why might a model with AUC=0.97 still be practically useless?

EX 03
🌡️ The Dynamic Threshold
Use the COVID-19 scenario to explore how the right threshold shifts with context.

Select COVID-19 Rapid Test. This scenario is intentionally different: the cost of FP (unnecessary isolation, economic disruption) and FN (spreading the virus) both matter substantially — and their relative weight changes with the stage of an epidemic.

→ At peak transmission (high prevalence, high community spread): drag the threshold low to minimize FN. Accept the false isolation burden. What is the recall at your chosen threshold?

→ At low prevalence (epidemic winding down): raise the threshold significantly. At low prevalence, most "positives" would be false positives even with a good test. Read the PR curve — notice how precision collapses at high recall when prevalence is low.

Key insight: The prevalence of the condition being screened is not just a property of the population — it changes the meaning of every threshold. This is why diagnostic guidelines get updated throughout an epidemic and why "one-size-fits-all" thresholds are almost always wrong.

EX 04
🗺️ Scenario Mapping
Open-ended: map all 10 scenarios onto the precision-recall spectrum.

Go through all 10 scenarios and for each one, note: (a) which error type is more costly, (b) what threshold you would recommend, and (c) whether the recommendation is obvious or genuinely debatable.

Which scenario has the most symmetric cost profile (FP ≈ FN in severity)? Which has the most extreme asymmetry? Which one do you find most ethically complex, and why?

Challenge: For the Loan Approval scenario — is the cost asymmetry the same from the bank's perspective as from the applicant's perspective? What does this reveal about how "cost" is defined and who gets to define it?

05

Discussion Questions

  • Who Decides the Threshold? A classifier's decision threshold is effectively a policy: it determines who gets flagged, who gets cleared, and who bears the cost of each error type. Should threshold decisions be made by the engineers who build the model, the organizations that deploy it, regulators, or the public? Does your answer change based on the domain?
  • The Asymmetry of Harm. In criminal facial recognition, the false positive (an innocent person wrongly identified) falls on a private individual, while the benefit of a true positive (a criminal caught) accrues to the public. Is it ever ethical to impose individual harm for collective benefit? How does the magnitude of each matter?
  • Precision vs. Recall in Hiring. An AI system screens job applications. If it's tuned for high precision (only recommend strongly qualified candidates), it may miss many qualified people from underrepresented groups whose resumes don't match historical patterns. If tuned for high recall, reviewers face a large pile of varied candidates. How would you choose the threshold — and who gets to make that call?
  • The PR Curve and Rare Events. For detecting rare fraud (0.01% of transactions), even a model with AUC=0.999 on the ROC curve might have precision of only 1-2% at high recall — meaning 98-99 flagged transactions are innocent for every fraud caught. At what precision would you consider such a system deployable? What does that imply about customer experience?
  • Connection Forward. In Era 5 you will study algorithmic bias — the systematic tendency of models to make different kinds of errors for different demographic groups. How does the precision/recall framework interact with bias? Could a model be "fair" on overall metrics but systematically harmful to specific groups depending on threshold placement?
