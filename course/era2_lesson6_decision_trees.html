02

Automating the Knowledge Engineer

Ross Quinlan was working on expert systems in the late 1970s when he got frustrated with the same problem everyone else in the field was running into: the knowledge acquisition bottleneck. Building MYCIN had required hundreds of hours of specialist interviews. Building XCON required a team of full-time knowledge engineers who spent their careers keeping the rules from contradicting each other. The rules were expensive to extract, fragile to maintain, and depended entirely on whether the human expert could articulate what they knew.

Quinlan's question was pointed: what if you didn't need the expert interview at all? What if you could give the system a set of examples — past cases with known correct answers — and let it figure out the rules itself? The examples were often available. The specialists who had made the correct decisions in those cases were not always available to explain their reasoning. Data was cheaper than expertise.

In 1979, Quinlan published ID3 — Iterative Dichotomiser 3. The algorithm was elegant: at each step, look at all the features you haven't split on yet, compute how much information each potential split would give you about the class labels, and choose the split that gives you the most information. Recurse on each resulting subset until the subsets are pure (all one class) or you run out of features. The result is a tree — a set of nested if-then rules — constructed automatically from data.

The key mathematical ingredient was borrowed from Claude Shannon's 1948 information theory: entropy, a measure of impurity or uncertainty in a set of labels. A set that is all one class has zero entropy — complete certainty. A set evenly split between classes has maximum entropy — complete uncertainty. The algorithm picks splits that reduce entropy the most, measured by information gain: the difference between the parent node's entropy and the weighted average entropy of the child nodes.

"The knowledge acquisition bottleneck is the fundamental problem of expert systems. ID3 addresses it directly: instead of asking the expert to articulate rules, we ask them to provide examples. The algorithm does the rest."

— Ross Quinlan, paraphrased from "Learning Efficient Classification Procedures," 1983

In 2016, the same year ProPublica published "Machine Bias," researchers asked a pointed question: how complex does a model actually need to be to match COMPAS's accuracy on the Broward County recidivism dataset? They trained a decision tree on the same data. The result was disquieting in its simplicity. The learned tree had essentially two decision nodes:

The COMPAS-matching Decision Tree — Learned from 7,000 Broward County Cases
This two-node tree matched COMPAS's accuracy on the Broward County dataset. COMPAS has 137 survey questions and a proprietary scoring algorithm. This tree has two questions. The complexity bought nothing in accuracy — but it did buy opacity. And look at what the tree learned: the most predictive feature is age. Age correlates with race in the dataset. The algorithm didn't know about race. It didn't need to.

The two-node tree is the lesson in concentrated form. Decision trees are interpretable by design — you can read every rule the model learned. But interpretability does not equal fairness. The tree learned to use age as a proxy for recidivism risk, and age is not race-neutral in a dataset shaped by decades of racially unequal policing and prosecution. The bias is visible in the tree. It was invisible in COMPAS. Whether visibility makes things better or worse is not a technical question.

Decision trees also introduced the first clear instance of a problem that will follow us through Era 3 and beyond: overfitting. A tree grown to full depth — splitting until every leaf is pure — memorizes the training data perfectly. Every training example gets its own leaf if necessary. The training accuracy is 100%. But the tree has learned the noise in the training data as if it were signal. Applied to new examples it has never seen, it performs far worse than a shallower tree that captured the genuine pattern without memorizing the exceptions. The fix — pruning — removes branches that don't improve generalization. But identifying which branches to remove requires a second dataset the tree has never seen: the test set.

04

Lab Exercises

EX 01
📐 Entropy by Hand
Compute entropy and information gain manually, then verify with Demo 1.

A node contains 10 patients: 6 high-risk, 4 low-risk. Compute the entropy H of this node using H = −p⁺ log₂(p⁺) − p⁻ log₂(p⁻) where p⁺ = 6/10 and p⁻ = 4/10.

→ Now suppose we split on "age ≥ 60": left child has 5 patients (4 high-risk, 1 low-risk), right child has 5 patients (2 high-risk, 3 low-risk). Compute the entropy of each child node, then compute the weighted average entropy after the split. The information gain is the parent entropy minus this weighted average.

Key question: In Demo 1 Mode B, what proportion split produces the maximum possible information gain for a given parent entropy? Is there a parent entropy value where no split can produce positive information gain?

EX 02
🌳 Build the Tree Greedily
Use Demo 2 to build a decision tree for heart disease risk — one split at a time.

In Demo 2, let the information gain bars guide your choices. At each node, split on the feature with the highest IG. Build the full tree until all leaves are pure or no IG-positive split remains.

→ At some point you may have a node where two features tie in information gain. Try both and observe the resulting subtrees. Are they equally good? What criterion other than IG might break the tie?

Key question: The tree you built is equivalent to a set of if-then rules — write them out explicitly. Compare this to the COMPAS tree shown in the lesson. Which is more complex? Does complexity correlate with accuracy here?

EX 03
🪓 Find the Overfitting Point
Use Demo 3 to identify the depth at which the tree starts to overfit.

Grow the tree step by step in Demo 3. Record training and test accuracy at each depth. At what depth does test accuracy peak? What is the gap between training and test accuracy at that depth vs. at maximum depth?

→ The "optimal" depth for this dataset is the depth that maximizes test accuracy. But in a real setting, you would not know the test accuracy during training — you'd only see training accuracy climbing. What could you use instead of test accuracy to detect the overfitting point during training?

Key insight: A tree grown to depth 1 (a "decision stump") is the most pruned possible tree — one split, two leaves. It has high bias (too simple to capture complex patterns) but low variance (very stable across different training sets). A full-depth tree has low bias but high variance. The bias-variance tradeoff — which gets its own lesson in Era 3 — is visible here in its simplest form.

05

Discussion Questions

  • The Opacity Paradox. The COMPAS tree is maximally interpretable — you can read every rule. COMPAS itself is a black box. Yet both encode the same bias (age as a proxy for race). Does interpretability help here? If you can see the bias in a decision tree, does that make it more or less acceptable to use than an opaque model with the same bias?
  • Learning vs. Encoding. Decision trees learn rules from data. Expert systems have humans write rules. The COMPAS tree learned to use age; a human knowledge engineer might have explicitly prohibited using age. Which approach is more dangerous, and under what conditions? When is it better to learn from data, and when is it better to encode knowledge directly?
  • The Overfitting Problem as a General Principle. Overfitting in decision trees is a specific instance of a general problem: a model that is too complex relative to the data it's trained on will memorize noise. Can you think of overfitting in a non-machine-learning context? (Hint: consider a student who memorizes past exam questions without understanding the underlying concepts, or a financial model calibrated exactly to historical data.)
  • Entropy and Information. Shannon's entropy was developed to measure the information content of communication signals — how many bits are needed to encode a message. Quinlan borrowed it to measure impurity in classification nodes. Is this borrowing legitimate, or is it a metaphor that happens to produce good algorithms? What would it mean for information gain to be the "wrong" measure for tree construction?
  • Connection to Era 3 — Random Forests. The main weakness of a single decision tree is variance: small changes in training data produce very different trees. Era 3 introduces Random Forests — ensembles of many decision trees, each trained on a random subset of the data and features, whose predictions are averaged. How does averaging across many high-variance models reduce overall variance? And what does this averaging cost in terms of interpretability?
06

Terminology

EntropyH — Shannon, 1948
A measure of impurity or uncertainty in a set of class labels. Zero when all examples belong to one class (complete certainty); maximum when classes are equally distributed (complete uncertainty). Used by ID3 and C4.5 to measure the quality of a split.
H(S) = −p⁺ log₂(p⁺) − p⁻ log₂(p⁻)
Information GainIG — ID3's split criterion
The reduction in entropy achieved by splitting a node on a particular feature. Computed as the parent entropy minus the weighted average entropy of the child nodes. ID3 greedily selects the feature with the highest information gain at each step.
IG(S, A) = H(S) − Σ |Sᵥ|/|S| · H(Sᵥ)
Gini ImpurityCART's alternative to entropy
An alternative measure of node impurity used by CART. Computationally simpler than entropy (no logarithm), produces similar trees in practice. Gini = 1 − Σ pᵢ². Zero for a pure node, maximum at 0.5 for a binary 50/50 split. Used by scikit-learn's DecisionTreeClassifier by default.
OverfittingMemorizing noise
When a model learns the training data too well — including its noise and idiosyncrasies — and therefore generalizes poorly to new data. A fully-grown decision tree has zero training error (it can partition training examples into pure leaves) but high test error. Symptom: training accuracy much higher than test accuracy. Fix: pruning, or stopping growth early.
PruningControlled complexity
Removing branches from a decision tree that do not improve generalization. Pre-pruning (early stopping): stop growing when a split doesn't improve accuracy on a validation set by a minimum threshold. Post-pruning: grow the full tree then remove branches bottom-up, keeping removals that improve validation accuracy. Pruning trades training accuracy for test accuracy.
Bias–Variance TradeoffPreview of Era 3
The tension between a model that is too simple (high bias — misses real patterns) and a model that is too complex (high variance — sensitive to noise in training data). A decision stump has high bias; a full-depth tree has high variance. The optimal depth balances both. Formalised in Era 3 — the decomposition error = bias² + variance + irreducible noise.
