The Neuron That Started Everything

In 1943, neurologist Warren McCulloch and mathematician Walter Pitts published a paper that changed the history of computing. They asked a deceptively simple question: what is the minimum thing a brain cell must do to count as "computing"?

Their answer was radical in its simplicity. A neuron, they argued, does exactly two things: it collects incoming signals — each weighted by the strength of its connection — and it decides. If the total incoming signal exceeds a threshold, it fires. Otherwise, silence. No memory. No emotion. No context. Just a weighted sum and a cliff edge.

In 1958, psychologist Frank Rosenblatt turned this into hardware. His Perceptron machine had 400 photocells as inputs, analog circuits to compute the weighted sum, and a single output light that switched on when the sum crossed a threshold. Crucially, it could learn — it adjusted its own weights based on whether it was right or wrong, using a simple rule: if I should have fired but didn't, increase the active weights; if I fired when I shouldn't have, decrease them.

"The Navy revealed the embryo of an electronic computer today that it expects will be able to walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be conscious of its existence." — New York Times, 1958

The hype outran the engineering. Eleven years later, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert published Perceptrons, mathematically proving that a single perceptron cannot solve a problem called XOR. The field lost funding for a decade. We now call this the first AI Winter. The perceptron was not the end of AI — but it was the first wall. Every neuron in every neural network today is still a variation on this same idea.

📐 The Formal Mathematics ▼ expand

A perceptron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, then applies the Heaviside step function:

ŷ = 1   if   Σᵢ (wᵢ · xᵢ)  ≥  θ
ŷ = 0   otherwise

Where xᵢ are binary inputs (0 or 1), wᵢ are the learned weights, and θ (theta) is the threshold.

A cleaner form absorbs θ by adding a bias neuron: an always-on input x₀ = 1 with weight w₀ = −θ. This gives ŷ = f(Σᵢ₌₀ⁿ wᵢxᵢ). Every modern network's "bias term" is a direct descendant of this trick.

Geometrically, the decision boundary is a hyperplane — a line in 2D, a plane in 3D, an (n−1)-dimensional cut in n dimensions. XOR fails because its "Yes" cases sit in diagonally opposite corners: no straight cut can isolate them without scooping up a "No" too. That impossibility is why multilayer networks (Week 4) had to be invented.

🧱  Why is this impossible? (Explanation)
This is provably impossible for a single perceptron.

Imagine plotting the four input cases on a 2D graph, with x₁ on one axis and x₉ on the other:

  (0,0) → Silent   (1,0) → FIRE
  (0,1) → FIRE    (1,1) → Silent

The two "FIRE" cases sit in diagonally opposite corners. So do the two "Silent" cases. A perceptron can only draw a straight line through this space. No single straight line can separate the FIRE corners from the Silent corners — any line that catches one FIRE point also catches one Silent point.

This is the XOR problem. Minsky and Papert proved this formally in 1969, triggering a decade of defunded AI research. The fix is to stack perceptrons — a hidden layer can compose multiple linear cuts into a non-linear boundary. That's Week 4.

05

Discussion Questions

  • The Weight Intuition. If you were building a perceptron to detect a stop sign in an image, which regions of the image would you give high positive weights? Could any pixel ever deserve a negative weight — and what would that mean semantically?
  • The Threshold as a Dial. Sliding θ up makes the neuron "harder to trigger." What biological phenomena correspond to raising or lowering a real neuron's firing threshold? Think about caffeine, sleep deprivation, anesthesia, or adrenaline.
  • The XOR Wall. You proved that a single perceptron can't solve XOR. But your brain solves XOR trivially — "exactly one of these two things is true" is obvious to you. What does this suggest about the architecture of biological neural networks versus a single neuron?
  • Historical Reflection. Minsky and Papert's 1969 proof triggered a decade of defunded AI research (the first "AI Winter"). Was that a proportionate response to the finding? If you were a research funding agency in 1970, what would you have done differently?
  • Connection Forward. The perceptron uses a hard step function — a cliff at θ. Later (Week 4.2) we replace this with smooth functions like sigmoid and ReLU. Why might a "soft" threshold be more useful for learning than a hard one? (Hint: think about what happens if you try to compute a gradient at a cliff edge.)

Extra Credit: The Digit Recognizer

✦ Extra credit — not required, but highly recommended before moving on

A single perceptron recognizes one pattern. But what if you need to recognize ten? The answer is beautifully simple: run ten perceptrons in parallel, one per digit. Every perceptron receives the same 7 inputs and computes its own weighted sum. The one with the highest score wins — a primitive form of multi-class classification.

The inputs are the 7 segments of a standard digital display — the same format used in calculators, clocks, and scoreboards since the 1960s. Each perceptron carries pre-loaded weights of +1 for segments its digit needs and −1 for segments it doesn't. Internally, inputs are encoded as +1 (ON) or −1 (OFF) so that both agreement and disagreement contribute to the score.
