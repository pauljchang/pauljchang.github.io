02

The Program That Shouldn't Have Worked

In 1966, MIT computer scientist Joseph Weizenbaum published a paper describing a program he called ELIZA. It was designed to simulate a Rogerian psychotherapist — the kind that reflects your words back to you as questions. Weizenbaum expected his demonstration to show the limits of computers. Instead, it demonstrated something far more unsettling about humans.

ELIZA contained no knowledge, no memory, no model of the world. It operated entirely through pattern matching: scanning input for keywords and structures, then filling response templates. If you said "I feel sad," it matched the pattern "I feel (something)" and replied "Tell me more about feeling sad." If nothing matched, it said "Please go on." That was it.

The reaction shocked Weizenbaum. His own secretary — who knew perfectly well the program was written by her boss — asked him to leave the room so she could talk to ELIZA privately. Psychiatrists proposed deploying ELIZA in clinical settings. Letters addressed directly to ELIZA arrived at MIT. People wept. People fell in love.

"I had not realized that extremely short exposures to a relatively simple computer program could induce powerful delusional thinking in quite normal people." — Joseph Weizenbaum, 1976

Weizenbaum spent the rest of his career writing about the danger of mistaking the appearance of understanding for understanding itself. His 1976 book, Computer Power and Human Reason, is still one of the most important critiques of AI ever written — and it came from the man who accidentally created the problem.

🔧 How ELIZA Actually Works — The Mechanism ▼ expand

ELIZA processes each input through three stages:

1. Pattern Matching. A prioritized list of rules is checked in order. Each rule contains a regular expression. The first rule whose pattern matches the input wins. If no specific rule matches, a fallback rule catches everything.

Rule: "I feel..." Pattern: /i (?:feel|felt|am feeling) (.+)/i Input: "I feel really anxious lately" Match: ✓ Capture group 1: "really anxious lately"

2. Pronoun Reflection. Captured text is "reflected" — pronouns are flipped so ELIZA can speak the user's words back at them. "I" becomes "you", "my" becomes "your", "am" becomes "are". This single trick is responsible for most of the uncanny feeling.

Before: "really anxious lately" After: "really anxious lately" ← (no pronouns here) Before: "my mother doesn't understand me" After: "your mother doesn't understand you"

3. Template Substitution. A response template is selected (cycling through several options per rule), and the reflected capture group is inserted at the {1} placeholder.

Template: "Tell me more about feeling {1}." Capture: "really anxious lately" Response: "Tell me more about feeling really anxious lately."

That's the complete algorithm. No semantic understanding. No memory between turns. No model of you, your life, or your emotions. Every response is assembled mechanically from the text you just typed. The intelligence is entirely in the eye of the beholder.

Lab Exercises

Use the chat + rule inspector above for all three exercises.

EX 01
🎭  The Illusion
Have a genuine, open-ended conversation with ELIZA for at least 10 turns — as if you were actually talking to a therapist. Don't look at the inspector while you type. Then go back and read the full conversation.

Reflection questions:

Which responses felt the most "intelligent"? Now look at the inspector for those turns — what rule actually fired? Was it a specific match or the fallback?

Which responses felt hollow or mechanical? What exposed the illusion for you?

Key insight to find: ELIZA is at its most convincing when it asks a question rather than making a statement — why might that be?

EX 02
🎯  The Prediction Game
Read the Rules Reference table above, then predict which rule will fire for each input below before clicking to send it. Click any row to send it to ELIZA automatically.
  • My mother was always so critical of me Which rule?
  • I dreamed I was falling last night Which rule?
  • You seem very cold and robotic Which rule?
  • I can't stop thinking about it Which rule?
  • Because nobody ever listens to me Which rule?
  • Are you actually a computer? Which rule?

After sending each one, check the inspector. How many did you predict correctly? Which surprised you?

EX 03
🧱  Break the Illusion
Find at least three inputs that produce obviously wrong, absurd, or context-blind responses. These are the seams in the pattern-matching fabric.

Things to try:

• Say something that refers to the previous turn: "That's not what I said" or "You just asked me that." What happens? Why?

• Use sarcasm or negation: "I feel absolutely fantastic and have no problems." Does ELIZA respond appropriately?

• State a fact: "My cat is named Biscuit." Then later say "What did I just tell you?"

• Ask a direct factual question: "What year is it?" or "What's 2 + 2?"

Key insight to find: Every failure you discover points to a specific capability that ELIZA lacks. What would a system need to handle each case correctly?

05

Discussion Questions

  • The ELIZA Effect. Why do people anthropomorphize ELIZA even when they know it's a program? What does this reveal about human psychology rather than machine intelligence?
  • The Therapist Choice. Weizenbaum chose the Rogerian therapist persona deliberately — it works better than, say, a news anchor or a math tutor. Why? What properties of that conversational role make pattern-matching more convincing?
  • Fallback as Strategy. "Please go on" and "Tell me more about that" are powerful defaults. Why do open-ended, non-committal responses hide ignorance more effectively than specific ones?
  • Memory and Context. ELIZA has no memory between turns. If you added only one feature to make it dramatically more convincing, what would it be? What's the minimum context a system would need?
  • Connection Forward. Large language models (Week 5) also produce text by predicting what should come next based on patterns. What's fundamentally different about how an LLM processes "I feel sad" compared to ELIZA? What's still similar?
