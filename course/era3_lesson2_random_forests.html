Many Weak Trees,
One Strong Forest

A single decision tree memorizes its training data. Hundreds of trees, each seeing different data and different features, vote together — and their errors cancel. This is not intuitive. It works remarkably well.

Leo Breiman and the Two Cultures

Leo Breiman spent his career at Berkeley being quietly contrarian. While most academic statisticians built elegant parametric models — linear regression, logistic regression, the GLM family — with interpretable coefficients grounded in probability theory, Breiman kept asking a simpler question: which approach actually produces more accurate predictions? His answer, accumulated over decades of empirical work, was that the statistical community had built a beautiful edifice that frequently lost to methods it considered theoretically inelegant.

In 2001 he published "Statistical Modeling: The Two Cultures" in Statistical Science, with discussion by some of the field's most eminent figures. Breiman argued that statisticians operated from a "data modeling culture" — you assume the data was generated by a stochastic model, estimate the model's parameters, and use the model for prediction. Machine learning operated from an "algorithmic modeling culture" — you treat the data generating process as unknown and use algorithms that optimize directly for predictive accuracy. The two cultures made different bets about what mattered, and the algorithmic culture was winning on real problems. The paper caused a furor. It was also largely correct.

Random Forests was Breiman's flagship demonstration of the algorithmic approach. The algorithm combines two ideas: bagging (bootstrap aggregating, which he had developed in 1994) and random feature selection at each split (building on Tin Kam Ho's 1998 random subspace method). Neither idea is complicated. Together they produce a method that consistently outperforms single decision trees and, on tabular data, competes with far more theoretically sophisticated approaches.

"Bagging is a variance reduction technique. A single tree has high variance. Many trees, trained on different bootstrap samples, average out to something with much lower variance and similar bias. The surprising thing is how well it works."

— Leo Breiman, "Random Forests," Machine Learning, 2001

The algorithm has two sources of randomness, and both matter. The first — bootstrap sampling — trains each tree on a randomly drawn sample with replacement from the training data. About 63% of training examples appear in each bootstrap sample; the remaining 37% (the "out-of-bag" examples) are never seen by that tree and can be used to estimate its generalization error for free. Because each tree sees a slightly different dataset, trees that would otherwise be nearly identical become noticeably different.

But bootstrap sampling alone doesn't create enough diversity. Trees trained on similar bootstrap samples still tend to pick the same dominant features first — if blood pressure is the strongest predictor, most trees will split on blood pressure at the root regardless of which bootstrap sample they see. The trees remain correlated, and correlated errors don't cancel when you average. This is where the second source of randomness enters: at every node, instead of considering all p features for the split, the algorithm randomly draws a subset of √p features and considers only those. This forces each tree to find good splits using different feature combinations. A tree that cannot use blood pressure at some node must find the best split among the remaining features — and discovers relationships the blood-pressure-first trees never explore. The result is a set of trees that are genuinely diverse, failing in different ways, whose errors partially cancel when aggregated.

The number of trees is not a tradeoff. Adding more trees reduces variance asymptotically toward a limit — it never increases it. The out-of-bag error typically plateaus by 200–500 trees. The consequential hyperparameter is max_features: the size of the random feature subset at each split. Too large and trees become correlated again; too small and each tree becomes too weak to find good splits. The √p heuristic is a rule of thumb arrived at empirically, not from theory.

Lab Exercises

EX 01
🗳️ Why the Ensemble Wins
In Demo 1, examine which patients each tree gets wrong — and why majority voting recovers the correct answer.

Open Demo 1 and reveal all five trees. Each tree achieves 70% accuracy — mediocre on its own. Now look at the vote table for Patient 1 (Age=60+, High BP, no high cholesterol, non-smoker, true label: Low Risk). Trees 1 and 3 predict High Risk incorrectly; Trees 2, 4, and 5 correctly predict Low Risk. Majority vote: 3 vs 2 → Low Risk. Correct.

→ Now find Patient 6 (no high age, High BP, no high cholesterol, smoker, true label: Low Risk). This is the one patient the ensemble gets wrong — three trees predict High Risk. Why might a blood-pressure + smoker combination look risky to a shallow tree trained on a limited bootstrap sample? What additional feature would disambiguate this patient correctly?

Key question: Toggle trees off in the right panel of Demo 1. Remove the two trees that get the most patients wrong. Does the ensemble get better or worse? What does this tell you about whether the forest depends on any individual tree being good?

EX 02
🌲 The Boundary Stabilises
Use Demo 2 to find the point of diminishing returns — where adding more trees stops helping.

In Demo 2, drag the slider from 1 tree to 50 trees. Record test accuracy at 1, 5, 10, 25, and 50 trees. At what point does test accuracy stop improving substantially? What is the OOB error at 50 trees compared to 5 trees?

→ Notice that the decision boundary at 1 tree is jagged and complex — it memorizes the training data with many small regions. At 50 trees it is smooth and regular. Yet the 50-tree forest is not "simpler" in the traditional sense — it is the average of 50 complex trees. How does averaging complex models produce a smoother result than any individual model?

Key question: The OOB error estimate is computed using examples that were not in each tree's bootstrap sample. Why is this a valid estimate of generalization error — and why does it mean you don't need a separate validation set to monitor overfitting in a Random Forest?

EX 03
📊 Feature Importance Caveats
Use Demo 3 to understand what feature importance measures — and what it misses.

In Demo 3, note the baseline OOB error with all features active. Toggle off the highest-importance feature. By how much does OOB error increase? Now restore it and toggle off the lowest-importance feature. Does it change at all?

→ Feature importance measures how much each feature contributes to reducing impurity across all splits in all trees. It does not measure causal effect — a feature can have high importance purely because it correlates with the outcome, even if removing it from the real world would have no effect. Can you think of a medical feature that might show high importance for cardiac risk prediction but is not itself causal?

Key question: If Age and Blood Pressure are both important for cardiac risk and are correlated with each other (older patients tend to have higher blood pressure), how might this affect their individual importance scores? Would removing Age increase BP's importance? This is the problem of feature importance in the presence of correlated features — the importance gets "shared" in unpredictable ways.

05

Discussion Questions

  • The Two Cultures. Breiman's 2001 paper argued that statistical modeling (fitting interpretable parametric models) and algorithmic modeling (optimizing for prediction accuracy) were genuinely different intellectual cultures that made different bets about what mattered. Twenty years later, which culture won? Are there domains where the statistical modeling culture's emphasis on interpretability and uncertainty quantification still matters more than raw predictive accuracy?
  • Interpretability vs. Accuracy. A single decision tree is fully interpretable — you can read every rule it learned. A Random Forest with 500 trees is not — you can extract feature importances, but you cannot read "the model." For medical diagnosis, legal decisions, or loan approvals, should interpretability be a hard requirement? Does the right answer depend on whether the model is making recommendations or decisions?
  • Why Does Averaging Work? The mathematical justification for bagging is that averaging reduces variance without changing bias, provided the models are uncorrelated. But in practice, trees trained on bootstrap samples are never perfectly uncorrelated. Why does the method still work well despite imperfect decorrelation? What is the practical limit — how correlated do models need to be before averaging stops helping?
  • Tabular Data's Last Stand. Random Forests and gradient boosted trees (the next lesson) consistently outperform deep neural networks on tabular data — structured data in rows and columns. This is surprising given that neural networks dominate on images, text, and audio. What property of tabular data might make tree-based ensembles particularly well suited to it?
  • Connection to Crowdsourced Intelligence. The Wikipedia community, prediction markets, and jury systems all aggregate judgments from many imperfect individuals and frequently produce better results than any individual expert. Is this the same phenomenon as ensemble averaging, or is it fundamentally different? What are the conditions under which crowds are wiser than experts — and when are they not?

Terminology

Bootstrap SamplingSample with replacement
Drawing n samples with replacement from n training examples. Each bootstrap sample contains ~63.2% of original examples (some repeated); ~36.8% are never drawn — the "out-of-bag" examples. The mathematical limit: P(never drawn) = (1-1/n)^n → 1/e ≈ 0.368. Creates training set diversity without requiring additional labeled data.
BaggingBootstrap Aggregating
Train multiple models on different bootstrap samples of the training data, then aggregate predictions (average for regression, majority vote for classification). Reduces variance without substantially changing bias. Most effective for high-variance base learners (deep decision trees). A Random Forest is bagging applied to decision trees with the additional twist of random feature selection.
for i in range(n_trees): sample = bootstrap(train_data) # sample with replacement tree_i = DecisionTree().fit(sample) prediction = majority_vote([tree.predict(x) for tree in trees])
Random Feature SelectionThe key RF innovation
At each node, consider only a random subset of √p features (for classification) rather than all p features. This is re-randomized independently at every node. Forces trees to find good splits using different feature combinations, reducing correlation between trees. Without this, all trees tend to split on the same dominant features first — correlated errors don't cancel when averaged.
Out-of-Bag ErrorFree cross-validation
An estimate of generalization error computed using each tree's out-of-bag examples — the ~37% of training examples not included in that tree's bootstrap sample. Each training example is out-of-bag for roughly 37% of trees; its OOB prediction is the majority vote of those trees. No separate validation set required. Closely approximates leave-one-out cross-validation but at much lower computational cost.
Feature ImportanceMean decrease in impurity
A measure of each feature's contribution to the forest's predictions, computed as the average reduction in impurity (Gini or entropy) across all splits on that feature, across all trees, weighted by the number of samples reaching each split. Provides a ranking of predictive relevance. Not causal: a feature can rank highly because it correlates with the outcome, even if it has no causal role. Correlated features share importance in unpredictable ways.
EnsembleWisdom of crowds
A collection of models whose predictions are combined. For classification: majority vote (each model gets one vote). For regression: average. The key property: if individual model errors are uncorrelated, the ensemble error is approximately 1/n of individual error variance. Random Forests achieve low correlation through bootstrap sampling and feature randomness. The number of trees controls how close the ensemble is to this theoretical limit.
