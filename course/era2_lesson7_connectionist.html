02

The Collapse

In 1986 symbolic AI was ascendant. The US Department of Defense's DARPA was funding AI research at unprecedented levels. Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry had committed $400 million to the Fifth Generation Computer Project, aiming to build Prolog-based AI systems that could translate languages, understand speech, and reason with expert-level knowledge. The UK had its own Alvey Programme. Expert systems were shipping at DEC, American Express, General Electric, and dozens of other corporations. A generation of AI researchers had built careers on the premise that knowledge engineering — encoding human expertise as logical rules — was the path to machine intelligence.

The first crack appeared in hardware. Lisp Machines — specialized workstations built by companies like Symbolics, LMI, and Xerox, optimized for running Lisp and Prolog programs — were the physical infrastructure of symbolic AI. They were expensive ($70,000-$100,000 each), proprietary, and fast at symbolic computation. In 1987, Sun Microsystems and other vendors began shipping general-purpose Unix workstations that were nearly as fast at symbolic computation, cost a fraction of the price, and could run everything else as well. The Lisp Machine market collapsed in a single year. Symbolics filed for bankruptcy. The hardware ecosystem that had been built around symbolic AI vanished.

The second crack appeared in maintenance. XCON at Digital Equipment Corporation had grown from 250 rules in 1980 to over 10,000 by 1988. A team of full-time knowledge engineers maintained it. Adding a new product line to DEC's catalog required months of rule updates, conflict resolution, and regression testing. The maintenance cost was approaching the productivity gain. Similar stories emerged from American Express (fraud detection expert systems), General Electric (locomotive repair diagnostics), and others. The knowledge acquisition bottleneck had been partially addressed by tools that automated rule extraction — but the maintenance explosion had not been addressed at all. Rules interacted in ways their authors hadn't anticipated. Changing one rule broke three others. The brittleness that had been theoretical in Lesson 2.1 was now a budget line item.

"The term 'AI winter' was coined because, just like agricultural winter, there was a sudden frost. Funding dried up almost overnight. Graduate students stopped listing AI on their resumes. The field had over-promised and under-delivered, and the people holding the budgets noticed."

— Attributed to Roger Schank, Yale AI Lab, circa 1988

Japan's Fifth Generation Project ended in 1991 with a quiet acknowledgment that its central premise — that Prolog-based parallel inference machines would become the dominant computing platform of the 1990s — had not been realized. The project produced some useful research in parallel processing and logic programming, but nothing that resembled the original vision. The international funding consensus for symbolic AI dissolved.

While symbolic AI was contracting, something was growing in the margins. The 1986 publication of backpropagation by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams had given the connectionist camp a practical training algorithm. By 1989, Yann LeCun at Bell Labs had trained a convolutional neural network — which he called LeNet — on handwritten digit recognition with results good enough that the US Postal Service began using it to sort mail. This was the first commercially deployed deep learning system. It operated quietly, processing millions of ZIP codes per day, while the academic debate between symbolists and connectionists continued in conference proceedings and journal pages.

The debate had a genuine intellectual core. Jerry Fodor and Zenon Pylyshyn published "Connectionism and Cognitive Architecture" in 1988, arguing that neural networks were fundamentally incapable of supporting structured, compositional thought. Their central argument was systematicity: any cognitive system capable of thinking "John loves Mary" must also be capable of thinking "Mary loves John" — not because it has seen this particular sentence, but because it understands the relational structure. Symbolic systems have this property by design — the relation loves(john, mary) immediately supports the query loves(mary, john) → false. Distributed representations in neural networks, Fodor and Pylyshyn argued, could not provide this structural guarantee.

The connectionists — Rumelhart, McClelland, Smolensky — responded that systematicity was an empirical property that neural networks could learn, not a structural requirement that only symbolic systems could satisfy. Paul Smolensky argued for "tensor product representations" that could encode compositional structure in distributed form. The debate was sophisticated and unresolved. Neural networks did not win the argument. They won the benchmarks — and eventually, that mattered more.

Lab Exercises

EX 01
📉 The Quadratic Wall
Use Demo 1 to find the rule count where maintenance becomes untenable — and estimate what XCON required.

Add rules in Demo 1 until you reach 50. Note the number of potential interactions. At what rule count does the interaction count exceed 1,000? At what count does it exceed 10,000?

→ XCON had approximately 10,000 rules at its peak. Use the formula n(n−1)/2 to compute the number of pairwise rule interactions. If each interaction takes 1 second to manually verify, how many person-years would it take to audit all interactions once?

Key question: This quadratic growth is why large knowledge bases become unmaintainable. Can you think of a software engineering analogy — another system where complexity grows quadratically with the number of components and eventually becomes unmanageable?

EX 02
⚖️ The Systematicity Argument
Use Demo 2 to probe the limits of pattern matching — and think through what Fodor and Pylyshyn were really claiming.

In Demo 2, try: (1) loves(john, mary) — both systems should succeed. (2) loves(mary, john) — the symbolic system correctly returns false; the pattern matcher fails. (3) loves(alice, bob) — a query about names not in the training data. (4) loves_someone(bob) — tests whether the system understands the derived rule.

→ The pattern matcher fails on queries 2–4 because it only knows what it has explicitly seen. The symbolic system succeeds because it understands the structure of the relation. This is Fodor and Pylyshyn's point: systematicity requires structural representation, not just pattern storage.

Key question: Modern large language models (GPT, Claude) are, at their core, very sophisticated pattern matchers trained on vast amounts of text. Do they exhibit systematicity? Try asking a language model whether "mary loves john" follows from "john loves mary" — does it get it right? Does that mean it understands the relation, or that it has seen enough similar patterns?

EX 03
🌅 Reading the Transition
Use Demo 3 to trace the exact moment when symbolic AI's decline and connectionism's rise intersected.

Click through all events in Demo 3's timeline in chronological order. Identify: (1) the last major symbolic AI investment before the collapse, (2) the first connectionist result that produced real commercial value, (3) the event that most clearly marks the end of symbolic AI's dominance.

→ Notice that the connectionist revival did not happen because symbolic AI was defeated in debate. It happened because connectionist systems produced better results on practical tasks. What does this suggest about how scientific paradigm shifts actually occur — is it logical argument or empirical demonstration that drives change?

Key insight: The Fodor-Pylyshyn argument was never formally refuted. Many researchers believe it identified a genuine limitation that modern neural networks still have. Yet neural networks dominate AI in 2025. Scientific fields do not always resolve their foundational debates — they often simply move on when one approach becomes overwhelmingly more productive.

05

Discussion Questions

  • Hype and Winter. The second AI winter, like the first, was partly caused by over-promising. Expert systems were sold as general-purpose reasoning engines; they turned out to be narrow, brittle tools that required enormous maintenance. What structural features of the AI research and funding ecosystem made over-promising likely? Do you see the same dynamics in current AI development?
  • The Unresolved Debate. Fodor and Pylyshyn's systematicity argument was never formally refuted. Some researchers argue that large language models exhibit emergent systematicity that their architects didn't build in. Others argue that apparent systematicity in LLMs is sophisticated pattern matching over a large enough corpus. What evidence would you need to settle this question? Can it be settled empirically?
  • The Quiet Revolution. LeCun's LeNet was sorting US mail by 1989 while academic AI was still debating symbolic vs. connectionist approaches. The deployment happened quietly, in a domain (postal sorting) that was not considered prestigious AI. What does this suggest about where AI progress actually happens — in academic debate or in applied domains that most researchers don't pay attention to?
  • Japan's Bet. The Fifth Generation Computer Project was a rational bet given what was known in 1982: symbolic AI was producing results, Prolog was expressive, parallel hardware was coming. The bet failed. In retrospect, what information was available in 1982 that should have made the bet seem riskier? Is there a lesson here about how to evaluate large-scale technology bets?
  • Connection to Era 3. The statistical learning revolution that dominates Era 3 was, in part, a pragmatic response to the failures of symbolic AI: instead of encoding knowledge, learn from data; instead of brittle rules, use probabilistic models that degrade gracefully. But statistical learning has its own failure modes — distribution shift, spurious correlations, opacity. In what ways does Era 3 repeat the pattern of Era 2 at a higher level of sophistication?
06

Terminology

AI WinterSecond: 1987–1993
A period of significantly reduced funding, interest, and optimism in AI research following unmet expectations. The second AI winter was triggered by the collapse of the Lisp Machine hardware market (1987), the expert system maintenance crisis (1988), and the failure of Japan's Fifth Generation Project (1991). Contrast with the first AI winter (1969–1986) triggered by the Minsky-Papert critique of perceptrons.
ConnectionismNeural network paradigm
The research program in cognitive science and AI that models cognition using networks of simple, interconnected processing units — artificial neurons. Opposed to the symbolic AI paradigm, which models cognition using explicit rule systems and logical inference. The term was used in the 1980s-90s debate; today "deep learning" is more common but refers to the same lineage.
SystematicityFodor & Pylyshyn, 1988
The property of a cognitive system that can think "John loves Mary" if and only if it can also think "Mary loves John" — not because it memorized both, but because it understands relational structure. Fodor and Pylyshyn argued this property is guaranteed by symbolic systems but not by connectionist networks. The argument was central to the 1980s-90s debate and remains influential in discussions of what neural networks can and cannot do.
Fifth Generation ProjectJapan, 1982–1991
A Japanese government initiative to build a new generation of computers based on Prolog and parallel processing, aimed at creating AI systems that could translate languages and reason at expert level. Funded at $400M over ten years. Produced useful research but failed to achieve its commercial and technical goals. Its quiet end in 1991 contributed to the collapse of the international funding consensus for symbolic AI.
Lisp MachineSymbolic AI hardware
Specialized workstation computers designed and optimized for running Lisp and other symbolic AI programs. Built by companies including Symbolics, LMI (Lisp Machine Inc.), and Xerox PARC. Priced at $70,000–$100,000. When general-purpose Unix workstations matched their performance at a fraction of the cost in 1987, the Lisp Machine market collapsed within a year, devastating the companies that had built the symbolic AI hardware ecosystem.
LeNetLeCun, 1989
A convolutional neural network developed by Yann LeCun at Bell Labs for recognizing handwritten digits. The first commercially deployed deep learning system — used by the US Postal Service to sort mail by ZIP code. Demonstrated that neural networks could produce commercially valuable results. A direct ancestor of modern convolutional networks used in image recognition.
