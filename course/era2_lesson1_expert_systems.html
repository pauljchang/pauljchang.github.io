02

The Summer After the Winter

By 1970 the first AI winter had arrived. The perceptron was discredited, neural network funding had collapsed, and Rosenblatt was dead. But the broader AI project hadn't stopped — it had simply changed its premise. If machines couldn't learn rules from data, perhaps the solution was to give them the rules directly. Interview a human expert. Extract their decision-making process. Write it down. Ship it.

This was the expert systems movement, and its ambition was not modest. The goal was to capture human expertise and make it universally available — to give every rural clinic access to a specialist diagnostician, every small manufacturer access to a world-class configuration engineer. The democratization of knowledge through code.

The first demonstration that this could actually work came from Stanford. In 1965, Edward Feigenbaum and Joshua Lederberg began building DENDRAL — a system that could determine the molecular structure of unknown organic compounds from mass spectrometry data. The task normally took expert chemists hours of painstaking analysis. DENDRAL encoded their inference rules and could do it in seconds. It was published in scientific journals and used by practising chemists. This was not a laboratory curiosity. It was a tool.

MYCIN (1972–79)

Stanford. Diagnoses bacterial blood infections, recommends antibiotics. Performs at specialist level in controlled trials.

69% vs 80% specialist accuracy

XCON (1980–)

Digital Equipment Corporation. Configures VAX computer systems. Handles 80% of orders. The largest commercial expert system of the era.

$40M/year saved, 10,000+ rules

MYCIN is the case study that defined the field. Built at Stanford between 1972 and 1979, it was designed to diagnose bacterial blood infections — a time-critical problem where the wrong antibiotic could be fatal. It contained approximately 600 rules. In a 1979 blind trial, it was evaluated by ten physicians including specialists and faculty. MYCIN achieved 69% accuracy on cases where the accepted therapy was known. The average infectious disease specialist scored 80%. General physicians scored between 36% and 62%. The computer was better than most human doctors at this specific task.

MYCIN was never deployed in clinical practice. The barrier was not technical — it was legal. No hospital would take responsibility for a diagnosis made by a computer program, regardless of its demonstrated accuracy. But the trial results reverberated through the field. If a rule-based system could approach specialist performance in medicine, the potential applications seemed unlimited.

"MYCIN illustrates both the power and the problems of expert systems. The power: it demonstrably works. The problems: it took years to build, it knows nothing outside its narrow domain, and it cannot explain why its rules are correct — only that they are."

— Bruce Buchanan & Edward Shortliffe, Rule-Based Expert Systems, 1984

The commercial peak arrived in the early 1980s. XCON at Digital Equipment Corporation became the paradigm case for industrial expert systems: real money, real scale, measurable returns. By 1986 it had over 10,000 rules and required a team of full-time knowledge engineers just to maintain it. That last detail contains the story of the second AI winter in miniature.

Rules interact. Adding a new rule to handle a previously unhandled case might quietly break three existing rules that depended on assumptions the new rule violated. By the time XCON had 10,000 rules, no single person could hold the full system in their head. Debugging it meant trawling through thousands of interactions looking for the conflict. The maintenance explosion — the super-linear growth in complexity as knowledge bases grew — meant that expert systems did not scale. They were brilliant up to a few hundred rules. They became unmaintainable at a few thousand.

The brittleness was the other wall. Expert systems assumed that if something wasn't in the knowledge base, it was false — the closed-world assumption. MYCIN had no rules for drug allergies. A patient allergic to penicillin would receive a penicillin recommendation without hesitation, because MYCIN's world contained no allergies. It couldn't say "I don't know" — it could only say what its rules said. Outside the domain, it didn't degrade gracefully. It failed confidently.

Lab Exercises

EX 01
🐧 The Penguin Problem
Use the Animals scenario to discover why taxonomic rules always need exceptions — and how exceptions multiply.

In Demo 1, select the Animals scenario. Check: has_feathers, has_wings, can_fly. Run inference. Note the classification. Now clear and try: has_feathers, has_wings (no can_fly). What happens? Try: has_feathers, has_wings, lives_in_water, black_and_white.

→ Click the Penguin edge case. A brittle rule fires — the system gets part of the answer right and part wrong. Toggle the brittle rule off. Does the result improve? What rule would you need to add to handle penguins correctly?

Key question: The platypus has fur, gives milk (it's a mammal) but also lays eggs and has a bill. Try the Platypus edge case. The system classifies it correctly — but only because a specific platypus rule exists. What does this imply about the scalability of rule-based classification? How many special-case rules would you need for all ~8.7 million known animal species?

EX 02
💊 The Penicillin Moment
Reproduce the MYCIN drug allergy failure — the most instructive brittleness case in expert systems history.

Select the Infection Diagnosis scenario. Set: fever, stiff_neck, confusion, headache. Run inference. The system correctly identifies bacterial meningitis and recommends penicillin.

→ Now also check allergic_to_penicillin. Run inference again. Read the alert that appears. The recommendation has not changed. This is not a bug — it is the system working exactly as designed. It has no rule for drug allergies, therefore drug allergies do not exist in its world.

→ Try the Malaria (no travel) edge case. The patient has fever, chills, and body aches — the system diagnoses flu. The travel history that would change the diagnosis is absent from the facts, and the system has no way to ask for it.

Key insight: Both failures are versions of the closed-world assumption: the system treats absence of information as absence of the thing. A human doctor would say "I need to ask about allergies before prescribing" and "Has this patient been abroad recently?" The expert system cannot ask questions it wasn't programmed to ask.

EX 03
⚙️ Forward vs. Backward
Use Demo 2 to understand when each chaining strategy is appropriate.

In Demo 2, select Animals in Forward mode. Choose a goal (e.g. "Robin facts") and step through. The engine starts with known facts and chains forward, firing every applicable rule until it reaches a classification.

→ Switch to Infection Diagnosis in Backward mode. Choose "bacterial_meningitis" as the goal. Step through — the engine starts from the hypothesis and works backward, collecting the evidence needed to prove it. Notice that it only pursues evidence relevant to the hypothesis, ignoring the rest of the knowledge base.

Key question: Forward chaining fires all applicable rules and produces all conclusions. Backward chaining only fires rules relevant to the current goal. For a knowledge base with 600 rules, when would forward chaining be impractically slow? When would backward chaining fail to find a correct answer?

05

Discussion Questions

  • The Knowledge Acquisition Bottleneck. Building MYCIN required hundreds of hours of interviews with infectious disease specialists. The experts often contradicted each other and struggled to articulate rules they followed intuitively. Much expertise is tacit — it cannot be easily verbalized. What does this imply about the limits of the expert systems approach? Can you think of domains where tacit knowledge is so dominant that rule extraction would be essentially impossible?
  • The Liability Gap. MYCIN was never deployed despite outperforming general physicians in trials. The barrier was legal liability — no hospital would accept responsibility for a computer's diagnosis. This gap between demonstrated capability and real-world deployment persists in AI today. What institutional and legal structures would need to change to allow AI diagnostic tools to be used clinically? Who should bear liability when an AI recommendation is wrong?
  • The Closed World. Expert systems operate under the closed-world assumption: if a fact isn't in the knowledge base, it's false. Humans operate under the open-world assumption: absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. In what kinds of applications is the closed-world assumption safe? In what kinds is it dangerous?
  • Rules vs. Weights. Expert systems encode knowledge as explicit if-then rules written by humans. Neural networks encode knowledge as numerical weights learned from data. Neither approach is universal. What are the genuine advantages of the expert systems approach that are lost in the move to learned models — and which of those advantages matter most in high-stakes domains like medicine or law?
  • Connection to Era 3. The failure of expert systems partly motivated the statistical learning revolution: instead of encoding rules, learn probabilities from data. But the brittleness problem didn't disappear — it became the distribution shift problem. A statistical model trained on data from one hospital fails when deployed at another. How is distribution shift analogous to the closed-world assumption failure you saw in Demo 1?
06

Terminology

Expert SystemRule-based AI
An AI system that encodes domain expertise as a set of if-then rules in a knowledge base and uses an inference engine to apply those rules to new inputs. Doesn't learn from data — rules are written by knowledge engineers through expert interviews.
Forward ChainingData-driven inference
Start with known facts. Fire every rule whose conditions are satisfied. Add the conclusions to the fact base. Repeat until no new facts are produced. Good for: "given what I know, what can I conclude?" Used in monitoring, alerting, and classification systems.
facts: {fever, cough} → fire rule 3 → add: {possible_flu} → fire rule 7 → add: {recommend_rest}
Backward ChainingGoal-driven inference
Start with a hypothesis (goal). Find rules whose conclusion matches the goal. Try to prove each condition of those rules (recursively). If all conditions can be proven from known facts, the goal is proved. Good for: "can I prove this specific hypothesis?" Used in diagnosis and query answering. MYCIN used backward chaining.
goal: bacterial_meningitis → need: fever AND stiff_neck AND confusion → check facts
Certainty FactorMYCIN's uncertainty model
A value between −1 and +1 attached to a rule or fact, representing the confidence that the conclusion holds given the conditions. MYCIN's pre-Bayesian approach to uncertainty. CF = 0.85 means "strong positive evidence." Not statistically rigorous but practical and interpretable.
Closed-World AssumptionCWA
The assumption that any fact not present in the knowledge base is false. Expert systems use CWA. Contrast with the open-world assumption (OWA) used in databases and knowledge graphs: absence of a fact means it is unknown, not false. CWA enables efficient inference but causes dangerous failures when the knowledge base is incomplete.
Knowledge Acquisition BottleneckThe hard part
The practical difficulty of extracting expertise from human experts and encoding it as rules. Experts often contradict each other, cannot articulate tacit knowledge, and forget edge cases. A primary reason expert systems are expensive to build and difficult to scale. MYCIN required hundreds of hours of specialist interviews for 600 rules.
