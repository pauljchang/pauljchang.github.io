The Theorem That Changed How We Think About Evidence

Thomas Bayes was an English minister and mathematician who died in 1761, leaving behind an unpublished manuscript. His friend Richard Price found the manuscript, recognized its significance, and submitted it to the Royal Society in 1763. The paper described a method for updating probability estimates as new evidence arrives — a procedure so simple it fits in one equation, and so deep that it remains actively contested in philosophy of science two and a half centuries later.

The theorem states: the probability of a hypothesis given evidence equals the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis, times the prior probability of the hypothesis, divided by the total probability of the evidence. In symbols: P(H|E) = P(E|H) · P(H) / P(E). The terms have names: P(H) is the prior — what you believed before seeing the evidence. P(H|E) is the posterior — what you should believe after. P(E|H) is the likelihood — how probable the evidence would be if the hypothesis were true. P(E) is the marginal likelihood — the total probability of seeing this evidence under all possible hypotheses.

The critical insight, and the one most people get wrong in practice, is the role of the prior. The posterior is not determined by the evidence alone — it is determined by the evidence combined with what you already believed. Ignore the prior and you commit the base rate fallacy: you treat the likelihood as if it were the posterior. This mistake has real consequences.

Consider a disease that affects 1 person in 10,000. A diagnostic test is 99% accurate — it correctly identifies 99% of people who have the disease, and correctly rules out 99% of people who don't. If the test returns positive, what is the probability that you actually have the disease? Most people guess around 99%. The correct answer — from Bayes' theorem — is approximately 1%. Out of every 10,000 people tested: roughly 1 has the disease and tests positive; roughly 100 don't have the disease but test positive anyway (1% false positive rate × 9,999 healthy people ≈ 100). So a positive result comes from 1 sick person and 100 healthy people — about 1% probability of disease given a positive test. The test is accurate; the disease is rare; the positive is almost certainly a false alarm.

"The most common mistake in reasoning about probability is ignoring the base rate. The test result feels like information about your specific case. It is actually information about a large population — and the population context dominates."

— Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow, paraphrased

Judea Pearl formalized Bayesian reasoning for AI in his 1988 book Probabilistic Reasoning in Intelligent Systems. His key contribution was the Bayesian network: a directed acyclic graph in which nodes represent random variables and edges represent conditional dependencies. The graph encodes which variables directly influence which others, and each node stores a conditional probability table. The structure lets you compute the effect of evidence at any node on beliefs at any other node — not just pairwise, but across a whole network of interacting causes and effects.

Bayesian networks were immediately useful in medical diagnosis. A patient presents with dyspnea (difficulty breathing). The network might contain: Smoking → Lung Cancer → Dyspnea, Smoking → Bronchitis → Dyspnea. Observing dyspnea increases the probability of both cancer and bronchitis. Observing that the patient smokes increases it further. Observing a normal chest X-ray decreases the cancer probability while leaving bronchitis unchanged. The network propagates all these constraints simultaneously, maintaining a coherent probability distribution over all variables.

The most commercially successful application of Bayesian reasoning in the 1990s was spam filtering. Naive Bayes — named for its "naive" assumption that words are conditionally independent given the class — computed P(spam | words) ∝ P(words | spam) · P(spam). Under the independence assumption, P(words | spam) is just the product of individual word likelihoods — a lookup table multiplication. It was fast, it worked on small training sets, and it was surprisingly accurate. Paul Graham's 2002 essay "A Plan for Spam" popularized the approach and triggered a wave of Bayesian spam filters that kept email usable for a decade before adversarial adaptation made it a genuine arms race.

04

Lab Exercises

EX 01
⚠️ Find the Crossover Point
Use Demo 1 to find when a positive test result is more likely true than false.

Start with prevalence = 1%, sensitivity = 95%, specificity = 95%. The posterior P(Disease | Positive) is low. Slowly increase prevalence. At what prevalence does the posterior cross 50%? This is the point where a positive test is more likely to be correct than incorrect.

→ Now try: keep prevalence at 1% and increase specificity toward 99.9%. What happens to the posterior? This is why screening tests for rare diseases need extremely high specificity — even a small false positive rate swamps the true positives when the disease is rare.

Key question: A COVID rapid test has sensitivity ~80% and specificity ~99%. During a wave when prevalence is ~5%, what is P(COVID | Positive)? During a low-prevalence period when prevalence is ~0.1%? Work through the math with Demo 1, then consider: should you interpret a positive test differently depending on whether cases are high or low in your community?

EX 02
🕸️ Explaining Away
Use Demo 2 to observe the "explaining away" phenomenon — a counterintuitive Bayesian behavior.

In Demo 2, set Dyspnea = TRUE. Note the posterior probabilities of Cancer and Bronchitis. Now, additionally set Bronchitis = TRUE. What happens to the probability of Cancer?

→ This is "explaining away" — once we know the dyspnea is explained by bronchitis, the probability of cancer decreases (even though we haven't changed the evidence about cancer directly). The two causes "compete" to explain the shared effect.

Key question: In a court of law, would "explaining away" be considered legitimate reasoning? If a defendant provides an alternative explanation for evidence, should it reduce the probability of the original hypothesis — even if the original hypothesis is still consistent with all observed evidence?

EX 03
📧 The Adversarial Spammer
Use Demo 3 to understand why Bayesian spam filters eventually failed.

In Demo 3, build a clearly spammy message using several spam words. Note the probability. Now add two or three strong ham words (meeting, report, schedule). Watch the probability change. Can you get a clearly spammy message down below 50% by adding enough ham words?

→ This is exactly what adversarial spammers learned to do: add long passages of legitimate text (random words from news articles, stock quotes, random sentences) to spam messages to dilute the spam signal. The Naive Bayes filter had to be extended with additional features (sender reputation, HTML structure, image ratio) to stay ahead.

Key insight: The independence assumption in Naive Bayes is exploitable. If words were truly independent, adding ham words would reduce spam probability. But in a real adversarial setting, the spammer can add any words they want. What would a more robust feature set look like that spammers couldn't easily game?

05

Discussion Questions

  • Frequentist vs. Bayesian. There is a longstanding debate in statistics between frequentists (probability is the long-run frequency of events in repeated experiments) and Bayesians (probability is a measure of subjective belief, updated by evidence). Frequentists argue that "P(hypothesis | data)" is meaningless because the hypothesis is either true or false — it doesn't have a probability. Bayesians argue that frequentism can't handle one-off events or scientific hypothesis testing. Which view seems more useful for AI applications, and why?
  • Pearl's Causality. Judea Pearl's later work went beyond Bayesian networks to formal causal inference — distinguishing "seeing" (observation) from "doing" (intervention). Observing that people with yellow fingers have higher cancer rates is correlation. Painting people's fingers yellow doesn't give them cancer. Pearl's do-calculus formalizes this distinction. Why does it matter for AI systems that make recommendations (e.g., "patients who took this drug had better outcomes")?
  • The Prior Problem. Bayesian reasoning requires specifying a prior probability before seeing evidence. Where do priors come from? In the spam filter, the prior P(spam) comes from observed email frequencies. In a scientific experiment, the prior P(hypothesis) is often contested. What happens when two Bayesian reasoners start with different priors? Can they converge on the same posterior if they see enough evidence?
  • Naive Bayes and Independence. The Naive Bayes assumption — that features are conditionally independent given the class — is almost certainly false for text. Words co-occur in patterns that are far from independent. Yet Naive Bayes classifiers outperform more sophisticated models on many text tasks. What does this suggest about the relationship between the correctness of model assumptions and predictive performance?
  • Connection to Era 3. Era 3 introduces methods like SVMs, random forests, and neural networks that don't explicitly model probability distributions. They produce predictions without telling you how confident they are. Bayesian methods produce calibrated probabilities — they tell you not just what they think but how sure they are. When is calibrated uncertainty more important than raw accuracy, and when does it not matter?
06

Terminology

Bayes' TheoremThe update rule
The fundamental rule for updating probabilities in light of evidence. Prior belief × likelihood of evidence / normalizing constant = posterior belief.
P(H|E) = P(E|H) · P(H) / P(E) P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|¬H)·P(¬H) % law of total probability
Prior / PosteriorBefore / after evidence
Prior P(H): probability of hypothesis before seeing evidence — your starting belief, based on background knowledge or base rates. Posterior P(H|E): probability of hypothesis after seeing evidence — your updated belief. The posterior from one observation becomes the prior for the next — Bayesian updating is sequential.
Base Rate FallacyIgnoring the prior
The error of ignoring the base rate (prior probability) when interpreting evidence, treating P(E|H) as if it were P(H|E). Classic example: a 99%-accurate test for a 1-in-10,000 disease returns positive — the probability of disease is ~1%, not 99%. The test accuracy is high; the disease is rare; the positive is almost always a false alarm.
Bayesian NetworkPearl, 1988
A directed acyclic graph (DAG) in which nodes are random variables and edges encode conditional dependencies. Each node stores a conditional probability table (CPT) given its parent nodes. Supports efficient inference: given observed evidence at some nodes, compute posterior probabilities at all other nodes. Pearl's belief propagation algorithm runs in linear time on trees.
Smoking → Cancer → Dyspnea Smoking → Bronchitis → Dyspnea
Naive BayesIndependence assumption
A Bayesian classifier that assumes all features are conditionally independent given the class. For text: P(spam | words) ∝ P(spam) · ∏ P(word_i | spam). "Naive" because the independence assumption is almost always wrong — but the classifier works surprisingly well in practice because it only needs to estimate the right class, not the exact probabilities.
P(spam|w₁,w₂,...,wₙ) ∝ P(spam) · P(w₁|spam) · P(w₂|spam) · ... · P(wₙ|spam)
Explaining AwayIntercausal reasoning
A Bayesian network phenomenon: when two causes compete to explain a shared effect, observing one cause reduces the posterior probability of the other — even without new evidence about the second cause. Example: dyspnea is caused by both cancer and bronchitis. Observing bronchitis "explains" the dyspnea, reducing the posterior probability of cancer. Unique to probabilistic causal models — classical logic cannot represent this.
