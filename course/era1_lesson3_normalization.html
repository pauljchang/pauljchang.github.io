The Same Idea, Three Times

Imagine two features in a housing dataset: number of bedrooms (1–5) and square footage (400–6,000). Both matter for predicting price. But if you hand them to a learning algorithm raw, something quietly catastrophic happens: the large-scale feature completely drowns out the small one. The gradient signal from square footage is thousands of times larger, so the algorithm spends almost all its effort adjusting the weight for that feature while barely touching the bedroom weight. The model converges slowly, often to the wrong answer, and the small-scale feature is effectively ignored.

Feature scaling is the cure. But which scaling method you choose matters enormously. Min-max scaling compresses everything to [0, 1] — elegant, but one extreme outlier (a $50M mansion) crushes every other house into [0, 0.01]. Z-score standardization centers the data at zero and scales by standard deviation — robust to outliers, but assumes roughly normal distribution. Logarithmic scaling is the right choice when values span multiple orders of magnitude — income, population, word frequency — because it compresses the vast tail of large values while preserving the structure at the low end.

Then there is a subtler kind of normalization: not of data, but of vectors. Unit vector normalization strips away magnitude and keeps only direction. The payoff is elegant: if two vectors are both unit length, their cosine similarity collapses to a dot product — one of the cheapest operations in linear algebra. This is not a coincidence. It is the reason modern search engines, word embeddings, and the entire Attention mechanism in Transformers are built on dot products.

Finally, quantization — the most aggressive normalization of all — intentionally reduces numerical precision to save memory and computation. Instead of a 32-bit float, store each weight as a 4-bit integer: 16 discrete levels instead of four billion. The resulting model is 8× smaller and runs 8× faster. Remarkably, accuracy barely suffers — because most of a trained model's information lives in the pattern of relative weights, not in their exact values. We will return to quantization in Era 6 when we have millions of weights to compress, but the mechanism is simple enough to understand right here, right now.

Lab Exercises

EX 01
⚖️  The Drowning Point
Use Demo 1 to find the exact conditions where weight drowning is most severe — and where normalization matters least.

→ Set Bedrooms to its maximum (5) and square footage to its minimum (400). What is the gradient ratio? Now reverse: Bedrooms at minimum, square footage at maximum. Which direction is worse, and why?

→ Switch to Min-Max normalization. Both sliders now produce values in [0,1]. What is the ratio now? Does it matter where the sliders are?

→ Switch to Z-Score. Move both sliders to their extreme ends. Does Z-Score equalize the gradients as well as Min-Max? Why might the answer depend on the position of the sliders?

Key insight: Min-Max normalization guarantees equal ranges but is sensitive to outliers. Z-Score normalization is more statistically meaningful but doesn't bound the output to [0,1]. Neither is universally superior — the right choice depends on your data distribution.
EX 02
📊  When Min-Max Breaks
Use Demo 2 to discover exactly what an outlier does to min-max normalization — and why log scaling exists.

The dataset has one extremely high earner (≈ $850K). Switch between Raw and Min-Max. What happens to the histogram bars for the 95% of people earning under $150K?

→ Now switch to Z-Score. The outlier is still visible but doesn't collapse the rest of the distribution. What does it mean for a value to be "3 standard deviations above the mean"?

→ Now try Log. Notice how the long right tail compresses into a near-normal shape. Why would this be useful before training a model? (Hint: think about what the model has to "learn" about the relationship between income and some output variable.)

Key insight: Log scaling is not just a visual trick — it reflects a genuine statistical property of many real-world distributions. Income, city populations, word frequencies, and earthquake magnitudes all follow approximately log-normal distributions. Modeling them on a log scale is more truthful, not just more convenient.
EX 03
🧭  The Dot Product Shortcut
Use Demo 3 to verify that normalizing vectors makes cosine similarity equal to a plain dot product.

→ In Raw mode, drag Vector A to a long position and Vector B to a short position pointing in roughly the same direction. They should have a high cosine similarity despite very different magnitudes. Now drag Vector A much longer — does the cosine similarity change?

→ Switch to Unit Length mode. The formula display changes from A·B / (|A||B|) to just Â·B̂. Verify that the cosine value is the same in both modes.

→ Find a position where the cosine is exactly 0. What is the geometric relationship between the two vectors? Find a position where it is −1.0. What does that mean?

Key insight: This is why modern search engines normalize all document and query vectors before storing them. The dot product is one of the fastest operations on modern hardware — it maps directly to a single CPU/GPU instruction (SIMD). Eliminating the magnitude division from cosine similarity isn't just mathematically elegant; it's a major practical speedup at billion-vector scale.
EX 04
🔢  The Precision Cliff
Use Demo 4 to find the bit depth at which quantization error becomes visibly large on the number line.

→ Set the weight to 0.733. Step down from 32-bit to 8-bit to 4-bit to 2-bit. At which bit depth does the error become larger than 0.05? Than 0.1?

→ At 4-bit, drag the slider slowly from −1.0 to +1.0. You will see the quantized value "jump" in discrete steps. Count the jumps — how many levels are there? Verify against the table.

→ At 2-bit (4 levels), what are the only representable values? If a model's weight needs to be 0.6, what does it get rounded to? What is the error, as a percentage of the range?

→ The table shows memory savings relative to 32-bit. A model with 7 billion parameters at 32-bit needs about 28 GB of memory. How much does 4-bit quantization require? What about 2-bit?

Key insight: 4-bit quantization (16 levels) sounds catastrophically imprecise. But remember: what matters is not the absolute value of any single weight, but the pattern of weights across millions of neurons. The redundancy in a trained model is so high that rounding each weight to the nearest 1/16th of its range loses surprisingly little information. You will understand why this redundancy exists in Era 4 (gradient descent) and Era 6 (QAT). For now: trust the surprising math.

Discussion Questions

  • The Preprocessing Gap. Feature scaling is one of the most commonly skipped steps in beginner ML pipelines. Why? What symptom would you observe in a model that was trained on unscaled features — and how would you diagnose the cause?
  • Outliers and Trust. Min-max normalization is broken by outliers, but removing outliers is often wrong (the $850K earner is real data). What are your options? When is it legitimate to cap, remove, or transform extreme values?
  • The Geometry of Meaning. Cosine similarity measures direction, not distance. In what situations would the direction of a vector be more meaningful than its magnitude? Can you think of a case where magnitude matters more?
  • The Quantization Tradeoff. We showed that 4-bit quantization preserves most accuracy. Does this feel surprising? What does it imply about how "redundant" a trained neural network is — and what does that suggest about the nature of what the model has learned?
  • Connection Forward. In Era 4 you will study Batch Normalization — a technique that normalizes the activations between layers during training, not just the inputs. Based on what you've learned here, why might normalizing activations mid-network improve training stability?

Terminology

Weight Drowninggradient dominance
When input features have very different scales, the gradient for the large-scale feature dominates weight updates, effectively "drowning out" gradients from small-scale features. The result: the model trains as if the small-scale feature doesn't exist, converging slowly or to the wrong solution. Cure: scale all features to a common range before training.
Min-Max Scalingnormalization to [0,1]
Maps every value to the range [0, 1] using the dataset's minimum and maximum.
x' = (x − min) / (max − min)
Weakness: a single extreme outlier changes min or max, compressing all other values into a tiny range. Best used when the data has known, bounded extremes (pixel values 0–255, for example) and no pathological outliers. → Compare: Z-Score (handles outliers better)
Z-Scorestandardization
Centers the data at 0 and scales by standard deviation. A z-score of +2.0 means "2 standard deviations above the mean."
x' = (x − μ) / σ
Assumes: data is roughly normally distributed. Outliers still affect σ but are less catastrophic than with min-max. Output is unbounded (can exceed [−3, +3] for extreme values). The best default choice for most ML pipelines. → Requires understanding: Normal distribution
Normal DistributionGaussian / bell curve
The symmetric bell-shaped probability distribution defined by mean μ and standard deviation σ. Appears throughout statistics and ML: weight initialization, noise models, central limit theorem. The z-score is only interpretable in terms of the normal: "1σ above the mean" means a specific percentile only if the distribution is approximately Gaussian.
f(x) = (1/σ√2π) · e^(−(x−μ)²/2σ²)
≈68% of data falls within ±1σ, ≈95% within ±2σ, ≈99.7% within ±3σ.
Cosine Similaritydirectional similarity
Measures the cosine of the angle between two vectors — 1.0 if they point the same direction, 0 if perpendicular, −1.0 if opposite. Unlike Euclidean distance, it ignores magnitude.
cos(θ) = A·B / (|A|·|B|) → Â·B̂ when both are unit vectors
The key insight: if vectors are pre-normalized to unit length, cosine similarity is just a dot product — eliminating the expensive division. Used in: search engines, word embeddings, Transformer attention.
Quantizationprecision reduction
Mapping continuous floating-point values to a discrete set of integers. A 32-bit float has ~4 billion representable values. An 8-bit integer has 256. A 4-bit integer has 16. Quantization introduces quantization error — the difference between the original and the nearest representable value — but enables 8× memory compression at 4-bit precision.
step = range / (2ⁿ − 1)    x_q = round(x / step) · step
Covered in depth in Era 6. Works because trained model weights are highly redundant — relative patterns matter more than exact values.
