This is the first lesson in a hands-on course that builds a working language model from scratch, one real concept at a time, using a tiny invented "caveperson" language instead of English — a stripped-down grammar of about a dozen words is small enough that you can read the entire training set in one glance, which is exactly the point: at this scale, nothing is hidden from you. Later lessons add position, attention, and a real training loop; everything you learn here about a 12-word toy still describes, mechanically, what happens inside a billion-parameter model — just at a size you can hold in your head.

What are we actually training? Next-word prediction — the same core task every GPT-style model is trained on: given the words so far, predict what comes next. Concretely, this model predicts each word from the average of every embedding before it in the sentence. That's a deliberate simplification — a real transformer would learn which earlier words matter most (that's attention, arriving in Lesson 3); here, every earlier word counts equally. It's just enough machinery to make embeddings do real work.

This is a genuine (tiny) neural network, not a precomputed lookup table — the embedding table and the linear layer are both matrices of trainable numbers, adjusted by gradient descent every time you click "Step." There's no separate giant word-by-word co-occurrence matrix being filled in behind the scenes. Curious how those two ideas relate anyway? ▾ They're more related than they look. Classical distributional-semantics methods (like GloVe) take the opposite approach: build an explicit word × word co-occurrence matrix — literally vocab-size × vocab-size — counting how often each pair of words appears near each other, then factor that matrix directly with linear algebra (no sentences, no gradient steps). It's a well-known result that this and the neural training you're doing here tend to land on very similar embeddings — two different routes to close to the same destination. The neural route is the one that scales to modern models; the matrix route is mostly of historical and theoretical interest today.
